### Summary

This paper argues that many philosophical problems about profund and deep questions arise from our use of imprecise natural language concepts rather than from the nature itself of metaphysical questions

Human language and concepts may be imprecise due to abstraction. We are able to have thoughts about people, events and objects at multiple levels and multiple relationships. This abstractions makes even harder to make consenses

"The success of mathematical thinking relies in great part on the creation of a language for expressing precise ideas"

### Concepts

**Ilusion of conceptual precision:** Defines that our mental and linguistic tools are too imprecise to make precise progress on deep questions. Natural language is loaded with ambiguity vagueness and conceptual representations that makes hard to formalize consensus. 

**Illusion of explanatory depths:** People believe that they understand commons systems like locks zippers or even pencils better than they do. When people are asked to explain how these things work, people realize their understanding is superficial

**Illusion of understanding:** People commonly overestimate their understanding on, for example, political ideas.

### Examples

"Existence is not an idea. Only its descriptions are", "Only that which was never born is deathless". The ability to form such meaningless thoughts that sound profound is one consequence of the illlusion of conceptual precision

**Examples of ambiguity of natural language:**
1) Existence: We think the word "exist" or "existence" is a precise concept that can determine some existencial truth, but it is actually a fuzzy communication tool. "It's not even clear that the physical world should be described as having independet objects or parts in any sense"
2) Sameness: The word "same" or "sameness" are context-dependent. For example in programming languages "equals" (=) is not the same as "is" (= =). 
3) The nature of mathematics: We think of mathematics as numbers and arithmetics, but what REALLY mathematics study are concepts and their relationships. Mathematics are used dfferently depending on different contexts (physics, music, natural science, etc). Mathematic knoledge are not always the same, rules like "the angles of a rectangle always equals to 180°" are just valide on a cartesian context, on concave or convex mathematics this rule does not always apply.
