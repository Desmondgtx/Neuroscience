## Random Walk Model

Example of a experimental task, where a tight cluster of 300 lines of various orientations is projected onto the screen. The people needs to respond if the 300 lines are predomminantly tilted to the left or to the right. This is called "choice reaction time"
People tend to process a small number of lines, sampling and sum this samples towards the "left" or "right" decision (top or bottom on the graph).

Graphics from [[Random Walk Model Script]]

![[Rplot.png]]
**Figure 2.1** Graphical illustration of a simple random-walk model. Each path corresponds to a single choice trial in which the participant repeatedly samples evidence from the stimulus until the sum of  the evidence is sufficient to make a response. X-axis is the time

When a stimulus presents information in favore of one or another decisions this introduces the concept of "drift". Having a highly informative stimulus (drift) permits more rapid extraction of information than staring at a non-informative stimulus that defies quick analysis

![[Frequency Random Walk Model.png]]
**Figure 2.2** Decison-time distributions when the stimulus is non-informative. Mean decision time consisting of the number of samples that had to be considered before the decison

![[Frequency decision drift.png]]
**Figure 2.3** **When DRIFT is implemented.** Predicted decision-time distributions from the simple random-walk model with a positive drift rate (0.03). **Even though the proportion of responses has shifted dramatically (such that around 90% are now "left" responses), the mean latencies remain indistinguishable**
